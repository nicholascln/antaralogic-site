The Collective Intelligence Infrastructure

Our Story

Where Human Instinct Meets Machine Intelligence

Somewhere between the fluorescent hum of a Cisco office in Kuala Lumpur and the quiet of a kitchen table in Johor, a question took shape that would not let go: what happens when the smartest technology on earth is built for individuals, but the most important decisions in life are made by groups?

For twenty years, *Nick sold enterprise technology across Asia-Pacific. Not from behind a desk—from inside the room where deals were made, where teams argued, where a single unspoken tension between two colleagues could derail a million-dollar contract. He watched groups succeed and fail, and he noticed something the technology never captured: the space between what people said and what they meant.

When large language models arrived—ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini—the world marvelled at their ability to converse, to reason, to create. But every interface was the same: one human, one machine, one conversation. The architecture assumed that intelligence was a private transaction.

Nick knew otherwise. Intelligence, the kind that builds businesses and holds families together and keeps small teams alive in hard markets, is not private. It is collective. It emerges from the friction of different perspectives, the patience of listening, and the courage to say what needs to be said in front of people who matter.

“Every AI on the market is built for the individual.
None of them are built for the group.”

AntaraLogic was born from that observation. Not as a technology company seeking a market, but as a market reality seeking the right technology. In ASEAN alone, there are more than seventy million small and medium enterprises—the kopitiam operators, the family logistics firms, the three-person design studios—all of whom collaborate daily with the tools of a previous era: group chats that bury insight in noise, spreadsheets that nobody updates, and meetings that end without resolution.

These businesses do not need artificial intelligence that replaces them. They need intelligence that understands them—not as individual users, but as working groups with shared context, unspoken dynamics, and collective memory.

The Architecture

AntaraLogic operates at what its founders call Layer 2—the contextual layer between the raw capability of AI models and the human relationships they serve. At Layer 1, companies like Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google build the engines. At Layer 3, the distant horizon of artificial general intelligence captures headlines and venture capital. Layer 2 is where the technology meets life.

The platform is built around a simple but profound constraint: the Power of 12. Groups of one to twelve members interact with an AI presence called Sage, which observes, learns, and surfaces patterns—but only when two or more independent data points from different members converge. Sage does not speak to flatter. It does not speak to fill silence. It speaks when something real has emerged from the group’s own interaction.

Twelve members generate sixty-six unique communication paths. That is enough complexity for genuine pattern recognition, but small enough that every member can hold every other member—and the AI—accountable. The constraint is not a limitation. It is the architecture of trust.

“AI is the great equaliser. A brilliant expert friend
that everyone deserves but few have access to.”

The Equaliser

Consider a café operator in Hartamas. He manages a team of five. His margins are thin. He cannot afford a management consultant, a business analyst, or even a full-time bookkeeper. But his team talks every day—about what sold, what didn’t, which supplier was late, which regular customer stopped coming. Those conversations contain intelligence. It is simply never captured, never structured, never acted upon.

Now give that team Sage. Not as a chatbot that answers questions, but as a quiet presence that listens to the rhythm of the business. When two staff members independently mention that weekend demand for iced drinks has shifted, Sage surfaces the pattern. When the owner mentions rising costs in one conversation and a supplier offers a bulk discount in another, Sage connects the dots. The café doesn’t need artificial intelligence. It needs its own intelligence—amplified, structured, and made visible.

This is the promise of collective intelligence infrastructure. Not AI that thinks for you, but AI that helps groups think together. The kopitiam operator gets the same calibre of insight that a multinational gets from a team of analysts. The three-person design studio sees patterns in their client feedback that would take a larger firm months to identify. The family business finally has a memory that doesn’t retire when the patriarch does.

The ASEAN Opportunity

Southeast Asia is not Silicon Valley. It is a mosaic of languages, cultures, and business practices where relationships precede transactions and trust is earned in person. The region’s seventy million SMEs are the backbone of its economy, but they have been largely excluded from the AI revolution—not because the technology doesn’t exist, but because it was never designed for how they work.

AntaraLogic is designed for exactly how they work. In circles, not silos. In conversation, not dashboards. In the language and rhythm of the teams themselves, not the sanitised templates of enterprise software built for Fortune 500 boardrooms.

The platform’s three products serve the full arc of this need.

The Mission

To build the infrastructure that makes collective intelligence accessible to every team, every family, and every community—starting with ASEAN.

*Nicholas Chin is the Founder & CEO of AntaraLogic. Born in Ipoh, educated in Newcastle upon Tyne, returned to work in Kuala Lumpur, moved down south to Singapore to expand his career, and now living in Johor.
